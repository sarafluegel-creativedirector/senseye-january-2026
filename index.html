Senseye Updates

Product & Platform Progress

January brought a meaningful leap forward in how we collect ocular data, one that directly strengthens our readiness for Phase III. We’ve implemented a key update to the ocular task that asks participants to rotate their phone during the assessment, allowing us to use the rear camera to collect data.

Improvements Leading to Huge Impacts  

This change has had a huge impact on data quality, allowing us to capture significantly higher-resolution imagery and improved frame rates, resulting in much stronger signal. These improvements have also helped address variability across participants. In particular, we’re now seeing much more reliable data capture for individuals with darker eye color, something that previously required additional downstream adjustments.

 

Real-time Impact

Cleaner data, fewer retries, and a smoother participant experience overall. To support this change, we’ve added real-time verbal guidance within the app. Participants now receive spoken feedback (for example, “a little higher”) to help them position the camera correctly. Early testing has been extremely encouraging, including with non-technical users.

Preliminary Voice Task Results

Using a single, simple user response question with a 60-second voice response, we are able to decompose the audio signal into features that differentiate PTSD groups.

Data & Architecture

Machine Learning & Modeling Progress

Alongside platform improvements, the machine learning team has been making important advances in both data scale and modeling approach.

Expanding Our Data Foundation

We are now training video-based point-of-gaze and pupil radius estimation models using the EVE dataset, a large, high-quality video dataset that includes labels for gaze angle, head angle, point of gaze, and pupil radius. Notably, the pupil labels were collected using infrared Tobii hardware, giving us exceptionally accurate ground-truth pupil measurements — even in low-contrast eyes. The dataset includes approximately 2.6 million high-resolution video frames captured with Basler cameras, effectively doubling the amount of eye data available for training. This expanded data foundation strengthens model robustness and improves generalization across participant variability.

Advancing Our Modeling Architecture

Modeling focus is on task fusion, an approach that combines data across tasks and increases the power behind a model. We have begun transitioning from more traditional machine learning approaches, which are fast to iterate and well-suited for early model prototyping, toward deeper learning architectures. These models are more powerful, particularly for task fusion, and represent an important step in moving from MP-oriented workflows toward DCT-intended analysis as we prepare for Phase III and beyond.

We are also exploring an early-stage end-to-end modeling approach, where raw video clips are used directly to generate PTSD predictions. While still in its infancy, this work reflects our longer-term vision for simplifying pipelines while preserving rigor and performance.

Phase III begins on February 23, and with it, a major transition for Senseye. 

This trial represents the start of the study that will ultimately be submitted to the FDA, and it’s the largest clinical trial of its kind. Every decision we’re making right now — from protocol execution to participant experience — is focused on meeting regulatory-grade standards while operating at scale.

Australia Rebate

Our Advanced Overseas Finding has been granted! This came in record time — about 2 months instead of the expected ~6 months. What it means: the Australian tax authorities have agreed that all of our eligible clinical trial spend in both Australia and the U.S. can be included in our Australian R&D tax rebate claim. Because we have meaningful clinical spend in the U.S., this expands the rebate base significantly. This was a deliberate long-term move we started ~2 years ago when we set up the Australian entity, and it’s great to see it pay off exactly as intended.

Computer Vision

Contrast-Banded Pupillometry Performance Framework (Low-Contrast Eyes)

Improve our shared language around low-contrast eyes by building and labeling a framework to easily identify different contrast bands. We will then use that framework to better understand our capabilities and how different bands are represented within our dataset.

Key Result:

  • Contrast metric implemented: a reproducible contrast score in the range [0, 1] computed from sampled frames, where 0 = no usable contrast between pupil/iris and 1 = ideal contrast, available for analysis and reporting by end of Q1 2026.
Details on Pupil Contrast Banding
  • Five initial contrast bands defined (adjustable): initial bands set as [0.0–0.2), [0.2–0.4), [0.4–0.6), [0.6–0.8), [0.8–1.0] with documentation and examples; includes explicit note that band thresholds may be refined by end of quarter by end of Q1 2026.
  • Per-band performance reporting live: pupillometry evaluation reports include performance by contrast band using the appropriate metrics (e.g., MCC, AUC, IoU as applicable) with baseline vs. current comparisons by end of Q1 2026.
  • Initial per-band performance targets set (adjustable): starting targets established for each band with the intent that higher-contrast bands score well above chance and lower-contrast bands have reduced expectations, and includes explicit statement targets may be refined by end of quarter:
  • Band 5 (0.8–1.0): high target (e.g., ~0.8 “strong” performance level, metric-appropriate)
  • Band 4 (0.6–0.8): high target (near Band 5, metric-appropriate)
  • Band 3 (0.4–0.6): moderate target (lower than Bands 4–5
  • Band 2 (0.2–0.4): approximately “coin-flip”/chance-level target (metric-appropriate)
  • Band 1 (0.0–0.2): low expected target given RGB limitations
  • Targets are documented and tied to the chosen metric definitions by end of Q1 2026.
  • Band distribution analysis completed: analysis of historical sessions completed to estimate the proportion of participants in each contrast band, with a documented plan/assumptions for extrapolating toward global population distribution by end of Q1 2026.
